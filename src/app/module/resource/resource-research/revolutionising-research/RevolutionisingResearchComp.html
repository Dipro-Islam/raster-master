Revolutionising research with the 3-G Model

The Three-Generation Research Model introduced by Rastar Master exemplifies a unique, democratic approach to community-driven research. This model brings together three generations—children, parents, and grandparents—each contributing distinct perspectives and invaluable real-life experiences. The essence of this approach lies in the belief that research is not solely the domain of scholars or professionals; rather, it’s an inclusive, community-centered activity where every voice matters. Lead researchers and scholar teams guide the process, but the real power comes from Indigenous community members, whose daily interactions and insights enrich the research with authenticity and immediacy. Reversal learning in the Three-Generation Model is a powerful dynamic that enriches the traditional flow of knowledge by facilitating a bidirectional learning experience among children, parents, and grandparents. In this model, younger generations are not just passive recipients of wisdom; instead, they play active roles in teaching new skills and perspectives to the older generations, particularly in areas like digital literacy, modern education, and innovative problem-solving techniques.

For instance, children adept at using technology often guide their parents and grandparents in navigating digital tools, thereby enhancing the family’s collective skill set and adaptability. This process is crucial for empowering older generations to stay connected and engaged in an increasingly digital world, bridging generational gaps and fostering mutual respect. At the same time, parents and grandparents impart life lessons, cultural knowledge, and practical skills rooted in experience, creating a reciprocal learning environment that builds resilience, adaptability, and collaboration. By promoting reversal learning, the Three-Generation Model fosters a supportive, interdependent community where knowledge flows in multiple directions, making the learning process richer and more relevant for everyone involved.For instance, a mother’s intuitive understanding of her child’s needs or a grandparent’s knowledge of local traditions and health practices serve as forms of evidence often absent from formal research. This grassroots model transcends conventional frameworks by validating life skills, experiential insights, and real-time observations as critical data. Each generation’s unique contribution ensures a comprehensive, interwoven narrative, producing findings that resonate deeply with the community. By democratizing research, the Three-Generation Model not only strengthens the quality and relevance of evidence but also promotes gender equity, empowering women and youth to lead in discovering and addressing their community’s needs.
